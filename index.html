Episode One — Lima

Lima Bound

18th March, 1921  |  Hotel Mori • Bar Cordano • Lima Hospital

Lima, March 1921. The city arrives before anyone is ready for it. They came from different corners of the world, drawn by a telegram and the promise of something worth finding. Dr. Rune Dreyer, who bade farewell to his devoted companion Ox at a Copenhagen train station and swatted away a journalist too ignorant to know the difference between Lima and Madagascar. Ilia Chornovil , Georgian ex-soldier who left a Bronx flophouse and a neighbour named Mario waving him off from the stoop, carrying the particular luggage of a man who has driven trucks he was not supposed to ask about. Arthur James, slipping away from a smoky Chicago anarchist's club with little more than his hat, his corncob pipe, and a carefully unspecified reason for needing to leave town quickly. And Francois Bellatier, photographer, Lima local, wearing a silk scarf high around her neck at a temperature that did not require it.

The city met them with heat, mule carts, and Petronila — the landlady of the Hotel Mori — who handed out room keys with the practised efficiency of a woman who has long since stopped expecting guests to be straightforward. They had time to spare before their evening appointment. The doctor acquired medical supplies through a combination of broken Latin and enthusiastic mime. The others found an elderly man and his llama in a sun-warmed plaza, and left wearing the finest alpaca wool Lima had to offer, Francois insisting on overpaying on principle.

That evening they found the Bar Cordano exactly where they had been told to find it, candles on the tables and the sun going down warm over the city. Augustus Larkin was already there — mid-forties, but wearing it badly, a suit that had recently become roomier than intended. Beside him, a Peruvian man called Mendoza who looked at the table, at the wall, at anything except the African-American gentleman sitting opposite him, who introduced himself as Jesse Hughes and watched everything with the steady attention of someone taking notes.

Larkin was enthusiastic in the way of men who have staked everything on a single outcome. A pyramid. Southern Peru, near Lake Titicaca. Artefacts from a farmer near Puno — a pendant and a golden cup — that had sent him south with trucks, dynamite, and a hired crew. Dr. Dreyer noted the pallor, the careful sips of water over alcohol, the slight tremor when Larkin set his glass down too hard. Opium withdrawal. A man burning through his last good days on a final expedition.

Later, after Larkin and Mendoza had departed into the Lima night, Jesse Hughes dropped the performance. His real name was Jackson Elias. He was an author, not an associate, and the pendant and the cup were from different centuries entirely — no chance they came from the same site. Someone had sold Larkin a story, and Mendoza was the most likely author of it. He lit his pipe and smiled the smile of a man who finds mysteries more invigorating than alarming.

They walked back to the Hotel Mori later than intended, Jackson among them. In the lobby, Petronila was mopping the floor with a cigarette in her mouth. She looked up without breaking rhythm.

"She has been waiting two hours. By the window."

The woman in the chair was perhaps thirty-five, dressed for travel and movement, sitting with the deliberate stillness of someone in whom stillness requires effort. One hand rested in her lap. The other was pressed against her left side, and the fingers were dark. Dr. Dreyer saw it from across the room — the blood soaked through, the waxen grey at the edges of her face, the sweat at her temples she hadn't bothered to wipe away. Her eyes, however, were entirely alert. She had been waiting, and now she was done waiting.

She said Jackson's real name. She produced a cloth-wrapped package and held it out with a steady hand. Her name was Luciana Quispe Mamani. She had been carrying this for three months, she said, and she was no longer certain whether she had been following it or it had been following her. There was a woman at Lake Titicaca. Nayra. Find her before anything else.

"Whatever is in that lake — it has been in my dreams since I touched that thing. Every night. It knows I have it."

She handed over the package, and something left her face — the weight of three months of running. Then she went quietly sideways in the chair, and the doctor was already moving.

She was alive. She needed a hospital immediately and two hours ago both. The whole strange company of them bundled into the Lima night. Jackson unwrapped the package in the cab. A golden disc, ancient and deliberate, heavier than it looked. He held it carefully, the way you hold something that has already caused damage.

Outside the hospital, Arthur heard it first — the mechanical ratchet of an automatic weapon being readied. He had heard it before. Take cover. The street erupted. A burst of machine gun fire from the shadows, and then chaos — bodies dropping, scrambling, Jackson going down hard, Ilia coming up ready with his knife and nothing left to shoot at. The car was already moving, brake lights shrinking away into the city, the shot Arthur fired after it finding nothing but Lima air.

In the silence that followed, Jackson Elias lay bleeding on the pavement outside a hospital, which was either the worst possible timing or the best.

The disc was still there. No one had taken it. Monday morning. 8am. Hotel España. The trucks will be waiting.

Episode Two — Lima

The Karisiri

18th–19th March, 1921  |  Lima Hospital • City Jail • Museo Arqueológico

The street outside Lima's central hospital was still warm with spent cartridge cases when the doctor applied the first pressure dressing to Jackson Elias's shoulder. The nurses came running toward the gunfire with the resigned efficiency of people who have decided that Lima is simply like this, and the story of how three foreigners and one unconscious author had arrived at their emergency entrance became, over the following hour, something the night staff would be telling for weeks.

Inspector Rodriguez arrived with them — a burly, sharp-eyed man who swept curtains aside and asked questions with the brisk authority of someone who has decided that foreigners are a category of problem rather than a category of person. He looked at the assembled investigators — shot, bloody, and in Dr. Dreyer's case magnificently indignant — and landed immediately on the obvious conclusion that they had brought this on themselves.

The subsequent exchange was not diplomatic. Dreyer, who had opinions about everything and the vocabulary to deploy them at considerable length, delivered several. Rodriguez, who had not become Inspector Rodriguez by tolerating opinions from people he had already decided were trouble, delivered the response that situations like this generally produce. The cell door clanged. The doctor's jacket was removed. The protests echoed down a corridor that had heard worse.

Arthur remained at Jackson's bedside, wrapped in a hospital blanket, a cold cup of coffee balanced on his knee. He fell asleep. This was understandable. It had been a long night.

Ilia, discharged with three stitches and a practical view of his own injuries, walked out into the Lima midnight and hailed a cab by the expedient method of standing in the road until one stopped. The driver offered tours, a cousin with a bus, and — after the relevant financial signals were exchanged — a contact who sold a very good Lee Enfield rifle at a price that didn't require asking where it had been. It has killed many men, señor. No shooting my countrymen. The duffel bag it arrived in was conspicuously long, but Lima at this hour had seen longer.

In the jail cell, the hours passed in the way that jail hours do — slowly and with company you would not have chosen. Dr. Dreyer had exhausted his repertoire of complaints, threatened the Danish ambassador twice, and was drifting toward the kind of exhausted half-sleep that constitutes the body overruling the mind, when something moved in the dark.

It moved quickly. It smelled wrong. It came from the direction of the cell's other occupants, and then it was on him — a figure, apparently human, except for the circular aperture that opened where a mouth should have been, ringed with teeth in a pattern like a seven-petalled flower. The lamprey-mouth latched onto his chest with a grip like a vice and began, with horrible efficiency, to drain him — not blood exactly, but something more fundamental. Strength leaving his arms. The doctor felt himself becoming a smaller man of science, and the mathematics of the situation were not encouraging.

The other prisoners saved him. They pressed themselves against the walls and screamed one word over and over — banging on the bars until the guards came running. Rodriguez himself arrived with a shotgun and put several rounds into the creature's back before it fell. It took considerable doing to bring it down.

"Karisiri. Karisiri."

When it was over, the doctor sat on the cell floor with a wound on his chest in the pattern of those teeth — a permanent record — and a body that was measurably less than it had been an hour before. He noted, with the clinical detachment of a man whose brain continues functioning regardless of the circumstances, that Rodriguez was not surprised. Not by the creature. Not by the word the other prisoners were screaming. The inspector apologised — visibly effortfully — and walked out without answering the question Dreyer had asked.

Morning arrived. Larkin appeared at the jail with the air of a man sorting out a mess he had not created but had somehow become responsible for. Rodriguez handed over the doctor with the expression of someone returning a difficult object they were glad to be rid of. Outside, on the pavement, in full view of the street, the doctor opened his shirt and said the word loudly and clearly.

"The Karisiri, Mr. Larkin. I was attacked last night. In your city's jail. I find myself wondering whether you recognise the name."

Larkin's face did something complicated and fast. He got the doctor into the car. You have disgraced yourself and this expedition. Make a better impression on this fine country. We leave on Monday. The door closed. Mendoza's eyes, in the wing mirror, held Dreyer's until the car turned the corner.

By afternoon they had regrouped, cleaned themselves up to a standard the hotel would accept, and visited Jackson in hospital. He would be on the trucks Monday, he said, shoulder and all. A message was waiting at the front desk when they returned — museum paper, university letterhead. Professor Sanchez was expecting Mr. Hughes at 2pm Saturday. In his office.

Jackson was in no condition to attend anything. The three of them went anyway.

Sanchez was younger than expected, sharper, and not remotely fooled by three rough-looking foreigners showing up instead of the man he'd invited. But he was also clearly someone who had been waiting for an opportunity to say certain things about the Larkin expedition. He said them. Larkin was a grave robber with good financing. The artefacts were obvious fakes from different centuries — any competent museum would have spotted it immediately. And yet Larkin was no fool. The fakes were cover — a way to recruit legitimate people and give the expedition the appearance of archaeological credibility while he went after whatever he was actually after.

He mentioned, almost in passing, a manuscript. A conquistador named Gaspar Figueroa, sixteenth century, difficult to read. His assistant had been working on a translation in the storeroom. She had been gone some time. He apologised for how long she was taking and went back to talking about the pyramid.

Ilia went to find her.

He found her. She was emaciated almost to mummification, her face frozen in an expression that did not require translation, a circular disc of torn flesh on her chest in a pattern he had seen before — recently, on someone who was still alive. The storeroom door locked behind him. The smell of smoke found its way under the gap.

The fire alarm went off.

Outside in the corridor, a student was on the floor — arm broken, the bone used as a weapon against him. Dreyer applied first aid with the focused efficiency of a man who has decided the situation is beyond outrage and into simply dealing with it. Ilya kicked the locked door open, found the storeroom empty of anything living, and picked up two things from beside the body: a notebook in English — a partial translation of the Figueroa manuscript — and a fragment of worked gold, two feet long, three inches wide, ripped from something larger. Heavy. Ancient. Covered in symbols that matched nothing in any book he had seen, and everything about the golden disc already in his pocket.

He followed the blood trail. It led, with the directness of something that was not trying to hide, straight to the closed door of Professor Sanchez's office.

From behind the door, they heard the professor scream.

The trucks leave Monday morning at eight. In a university store room is a dead student and whatever walked out of that storeroom has just gone through a professor's door.

Episode Three — Lima

What Walks Out of Rooms

19th–20th March, 1921  |  Museo Arqueológico • Bar Cordano • Hotel Mori

The door came off its hinges in the way that doors do when someone with Ilya's particular experience applies a boot to them. What they found on the other side was this: Mendoza, with his back to them, pinning Professor Sanchez to the floor, making a noise that no human throat ought to produce. The lamprey-mouth was open. The feeding had already begun.

Ilya shot him. Arthur shot him. Between them they put enough lead into Mendoza to settle the question for any ordinary man. He took a rifle round and three .45 shots — one of them through the skull — and he dropped, and he was still, and they moved on to the professor and the burning building and the business of getting out alive. The disc of teeth on Dr. Dreyer's chest was a permanent record of what those wounds meant. They did not linger.

The sanity cost was immediate and unkind. Ilya, face and shirt still wet with what the shots had scattered, was overtaken by the compulsion you acquire when you have seen something that moves like a living lamprey do its work at close range: wash, now, thoroughly, until the skin protests. He set the professor down in the hallway and disappeared into a bathroom and was still there, running the tap, when Inspector Rodriguez arrived with his officers.

They brought Rodriguez back to the office to show him what they had done. The body was gone. The sword was gone. The blood remained, but Mendoza — three bullets in his skull — was not there. Arthur grabbed the inspector by the lapels and shook him and described the sucking noise and the feeding and the creature's face with the focused urgency of a man who needs to be believed and knows exactly how he sounds. Rodriguez removed the hands from his overcoat with the careful deliberateness of a man who has already made up his mind.

"Senor. I believe you. There is an infestation of these creatures."

"A body would have been useful. But I'm sure the professor can clear everything up."

He was weary. He knew this was not the end of it. The doctor, sitting in the corridor outside, watched an ambulance take Professor Sanchez away — and then, across the square, caught a glimpse of Mendoza walking. Walking steadily, one hand raised to his head, like a man with a headache. Not like a man with no substantial skull remaining.

In a cab on the way back, Ilya produced the documents recovered from the storeroom. The English translation of Gaspar Figueroa's Final Confessions — sixteenth century, written on vellum, now summarised in a student's careful hand — told a story they were already living. A Spanish conquistador. A pyramid southwest of Lake Titicaca. Tunnels inlaid with gold. A large section of that gold, prised from the walls in a night of exhausting labour. An evil sickness.

Figueroa's companions woke gaunt and ravenous and turned on him. One of them — Luis de Mendoza — caught up with him and attempted to devour him. Figueroa shot his friend in the head and fled. He made it back to Lima, wasted, little more than a walking corpse, and died the day after completing his confession. He had been hearing a voice in the dark. Ancient. Seductive. Promising eternal life, if he would only return.

Four hundred and eighty years later, the same Mendoza was still getting shot in the head, still walking it off, and still — apparently — heading toward the pyramid. Perhaps he wanted to suppress the information. Perhaps he wanted someone to carry him home.

They found Larkin at the Bar Cordano, entertaining company at his usual table. He dismissed the girls and listened, and the dismissal of the girls was the most alarming thing he did, because it meant he was taking it seriously. The bastard wants all the gold for himself, was his first interpretation, and they did not correct it entirely, because some suspicions wanted to settle in their own time.

Ilya slid the Figueroa notes across the table — the lines about the ancient voice, the promise of eternal life, the return to the temple. The doctor tried psychology. Five points of luck bought a hard success that said: not entirely truthful. Gold and money were not the end outcome. Whether the alternative was fame or something considerably worse was a question that would have to wait.

Outside the bar, the doctor collected empanadas and was followed. He had the extraordinary listen roll to hear large paws land behind him — not one pair but two. They spoke quietly and with the unhurried authority of people who have been watching the situation longer than anyone else in it. The hospital is protected. Your hotel is protected. Mendoza's whereabouts are known. If you have means to leave sooner, do so. The doctor asked whether the general population ought to be warned. They said that if the investigators were successful, the danger would pass. There was something they did not say after that. They were gone before he turned back to his empanadas.

Night came with the particular determination of nights that have been earned. They gathered at the Hotel Mori — Larkin moved from the España without much protest — and after food and the long accounting of a very long day, they went to bed.

Ilya fell asleep in a bathtub. He was floating, and then flying, and high above him a jaguar moved through the sky the way a serpent moves, sinuous and inevitable. Its eyes found him. His eyes answered. He was carried to a temple, and inside the temple was a tooth — ancient, gleaming, clearly meant to be somewhere specific, and equally clearly not where it was. He remembered that Luciana had also dreamed of jaguars. He sat up in the bathwater and looked in the mirror, and his eyes looked different.

Arthur had a dream that was built entirely for him: cities falling, explosions climbing into the sky, pure chaos, nobody in charge, everybody equal, everything burning. A cold dark hand rested on his, and the hand rested on a switch. Is this what you want? He failed the power roll. He failed it honestly. He woke up feeling, despite everything, empowered. There was a small smile on his face as he turned over. He was not sure he had put it there.

Dr. Dreyer also had a power roll. He failed his. He did not dream. He simply lay there and let the night pass over him, which was, given everything, probably the kindest outcome available.

The trucks leave Monday. Jackson will be on them. Luciana is still in a coma. Mendoza is somewhere in Lima, healing at a rate that should not be possible. Larkin could be seeking more than gold. Ilya's eyes look different in mirrors. Arthur has been shown a switch. Whatever is waiting at the pyramid has had four hundred and eighty years to be patient. It can wait until Monday.

Episode Four — Lima

Hunter of the Disc

21st March, 1921  |  Hotel Mori • Lima Hospital • The Racecourse

The Doctor was awake before the city was. He sat in the quiet of his room and wrote — long, spidery longhand covering page after page — setting out what he knew and what he suspected and what he could not yet account for. Three murders by three different means. No clear answer on who had shot Elias outside the hospital. The question of Mendoza's real purpose and Larkin — whether his interest in eternal life was a matter of academic curiosity or something considerably more personal. The pages filled up with facts and question marks in roughly equal measure.

He took Chema's cab to the hospital before breakfast, passing a side-street brawl without stopping. At the ward he found Luciana's dressings being changed and his medical skills told him what the nurses' prayers had not: this was no animal wound. It was a stiletto, precise and deliberate, administered by someone who wanted her alive long enough to talk. There was bruising at the throat consistent with a hand holding her in place. She had been meant to deliver a message before she died, and perhaps she had. The wounds offered nothing he could do for her. He filed the information away and collected Jackson Elias, who was discharged after his breakfast.

The cab that moved off into Lima traffic was Chema's cab but the voice from the front seat was wrong. The direction was wrong — southwest, away from the hotel, toward the racecourse — and when the Doctor said so, the driver said: "Where are you really going?" A gunshot bedded into the upholstery behind them when they repeated themselves. The man wanted the golden disc. He was willing to sit in that car all day with a tommy gun across his knees until he found out. To make sure neither passenger decided to become heroic, he had placed a pressure-plate device beneath the seat wired to dynamite and a dead man's switch.

Back at the hotel, Chema burst through the doors in a state of grief and outrage at the theft of his cab and told Ilya and Arthur the situation. Ilya ran upstairs for his rifle. They borrowed Chema's friend's cab and drove southwest, stopped a street vendor for directions and followed the wheel tracks to a storage area near the racecourse. As they tried to sneak up, Arthur trod on a cat, which announced them with the full force of its displeasure. A voice from the open ground told them to stay where they were.

The man they found standing in the open had a Southern European accent and a drum-fed tommy gun pointed at them from a very well chosen tactical spot. Not a local. Not a robber. A specialist of some kind, clean of documentation and identity, who had followed the disc from wherever it had started and was prepared to detonate everyone in the car rather than leave without it.

Arthur and Ilya withdrew and had lunch, leaving the Doctor and Jackson sweating in the back of the cab. On their return, Arthur rolled the disc across the floor, landing it halfway between them — but the disc began to move by itself. Arthur fired a shot through his coat pocket and the disc stopped. The kidnapper took a step back and opened fire into the cab, injuring the Doctor. Gunfire erupted. The kidnapper absorbed bullet after bullet but hadn't reckoned on Ilya's rifle shot putting a round through the centre of his forehead — releasing the dead man's switch on the dynamite.

The car exploded. The Doctor and Jackson Elias seemed to be vaporised but were actually thrown through the back seat as it detonated and blown clear of the wreckage. Ilya, to ensure the kidnapper stayed down, cut his head off. Chema mourned his cab. Inspector Rodriguez arrived, found the horrific scene, and with the Doctor off to hospital filled in the detail that the man they killed was Fausto Calderón, known as The Hunter. He delivered his warning with the controlled weariness of a man professionally accustomed to foreigners causing disasters. He wanted them out of Lima by morning at the latest.

That night the Doctor didn't rest well, lying in the dark thinking of the time that had passed since the Spanish flu took his wife and child two years before. The violence of the day had opened the old question: what any of it was for, whether he mattered to anyone, whether this expedition was purpose or simply distraction. The streets outside provided their own commentary — shouts, a cry, the sound of Lima conducting its private business at speed. He lay there and felt the city and arrived at morning no better for it. The others slept. Arthur and Ilya dreamed of the open ground where they had met the man known as the Hunter.

At eight o'clock the trucks rolled in. Three of them, diesel-smelling and heavy-laden, their beds stacked with food, limited medical supplies, and enough dynamite to considerably rearrange a Peruvian hillside. Arthur inspected the explosives, noted they were badly packed for mountain roads, and corrected this with netting. The trucks were named: Niña, Pinta, Santa María. Fifteen crew between them, three rifles, three drivers. The investigators divided: Larkin and Pelletier in the Niña, the Doctor and the interpreter in the middle truck, Arthur and Ilya riding with the explosives.

Eight hundred miles to Puno. Then the Andes. Then whatever is in the pyramid.

The roads in 1921 are not good. Will there be bandits? What about Mendoza?
