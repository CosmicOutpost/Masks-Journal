01
Episode One — Lima

The Golden Disc

18th March, 1921  |  Hotel Mori • Bar Cordano • Lima Hospital

Lima, March 1921. The city arrives before anyone is ready for it. They came from different corners of the world, drawn by a telegram and the promise of something worth finding. Dr. Rune Dreyer, who bade farewell to his devoted companion Ox at a Copenhagen train station and swatted away a journalist too ignorant to know the difference between Lima and Madagascar. Ilya, who left a Bronx flophouse and a neighbour named Mario waving him off from the stoop, carrying the particular luggage of a man who has driven trucks he was not supposed to ask about. Arthur James, slipping away from a smoky Chicago anarchist's club with little more than his hat, his corncob pipe, and a carefully unspecified reason for needing to leave town quickly. And Francois Bellatier, photographer, Lima local, wearing a silk scarf high around her neck at a temperature that did not require it.

The city met them with heat, mule carts, and Petronila — the landlady of the Hotel Mori — who handed out room keys with the practised efficiency of a woman who has long since stopped expecting guests to be straightforward. They had time to spare before their evening appointment. The doctor acquired medical supplies through a combination of broken Latin and enthusiastic mime. The others found an elderly man and his llama in a sun-warmed plaza, and left wearing the finest alpaca wool Lima had to offer, Francois insisting on overpaying on principle.

That evening they found the Bar Cordano exactly where they had been told to find it, candles on the tables and the sun going down warm over the city. Augustus Larkin was already there — mid-forties, but wearing it badly, a suit that had recently become roomier than intended. Beside him, a Peruvian man called Mendoza who looked at the table, at the wall, at anything except the African-American gentleman sitting opposite him, who introduced himself as Jesse Hughes and watched everything with the steady attention of someone taking notes.

Larkin was enthusiastic in the way of men who have staked everything on a single outcome. A pyramid. Southern Peru, near Lake Titicaca. Artefacts from a farmer near Puno — a pendant and a golden cup — that had sent him south with trucks, dynamite, and a hired crew. Dr. Dreyer noted the pallor, the careful sips of water over alcohol, the slight tremor when Larkin set his glass down too hard. Opium withdrawal. A man burning through his last good days on a final expedition.

Later, after Larkin and Mendoza had departed into the Lima night, Jesse Hughes dropped the performance. His real name was Jackson Elias. He was an author, not an associate, and the pendant and the cup were from different centuries entirely — no chance they came from the same site. Someone had sold Larkin a story, and Mendoza was the most likely author of it. He lit his pipe and smiled the smile of a man who finds mysteries more invigorating than alarming.

They walked back to the Hotel Mori later than intended, Jackson among them. In the lobby, Petronila was mopping the floor with a cigarette in her mouth. She looked up without breaking rhythm.

"She has been waiting two hours. By the window."

The woman in the chair was perhaps thirty-five, dressed for travel and movement, sitting with the deliberate stillness of someone in whom stillness requires effort. One hand rested in her lap. The other was pressed against her left side, and the fingers were dark. Dr. Dreyer saw it from across the room — the blood soaked through, the waxen grey at the edges of her face, the sweat at her temples she hadn't bothered to wipe away. Her eyes, however, were entirely alert. She had been waiting, and now she was done waiting.

She said Jackson's real name. She produced a cloth-wrapped package and held it out with a steady hand. Her name was Luciana Quispe Mamani. She had been carrying this for three months, she said, and she was no longer certain whether she had been following it or it had been following her. There was a woman at Lake Titicaca. Nayra. Find her before anything else.

"Whatever is in that lake — it has been in my dreams since I touched that thing. Every night. It knows I have it."

She handed over the package, and something left her face — the weight of three months of running. Then she went quietly sideways in the chair, and the doctor was already moving.

She was alive. She needed a hospital immediately and two hours ago both. The whole strange company of them bundled into the Lima night. Jackson unwrapped the package in the cab. A golden disc, ancient and deliberate, heavier than it looked. He held it carefully, the way you hold something that has already caused damage.

Outside the hospital, Arthur heard it first — the mechanical ratchet of an automatic weapon being readied. He had heard it before. Take cover. The street erupted. A burst of machine gun fire from the shadows, and then chaos — bodies dropping, scrambling, Jackson going down hard, Ilya coming up ready with his knife and nothing left to shoot at. The car was already moving, brake lights shrinking away into the city, the shot Arthur fired after it finding nothing but Lima air.

In the silence that followed, Jackson Elias lay bleeding on the pavement outside a hospital, which was either the worst possible timing or the best.

The disc was still there. No one had taken it. Monday morning. 8am. Hotel España. The trucks will be waiting.

02
Episode Two — Lima

What the Professor Knows

19th March, 1921  |  Lima Hospital • Hotel Mori • The University

The morning after Luciana collapsed, the Doctor went back to the hospital. The wound was not what it appeared — not a street robbery, not an accident. Someone had put a stiletto into her with professional precision, aiming to wound rather than kill, close enough to the vital structures to be a warning. She was alive because whoever did it wanted her alive, at least for a while. The disc was not on her when she arrived. She had already passed it on.

Jackson Elias, recovering in the next ward, was considerably less willing to stay in bed than the nurses preferred. He had questions about Mendoza — specifically, where he had gone after the Bar Cordano and what his connection was to the farmer who had sold Larkin the artefacts. The doctor had questions too. Between them they assembled a picture that did not improve on closer inspection.

At the university, Professor Sanchez had been making his own inquiries into the Figueroa provenance when something got there first. The investigators arrived to find smoke and chaos — the professor's assistant dead, the office ransacked, and Mendoza himself in the storeroom, doing what Karisiri do. The lamprey-mouth. The feeding. The particular horror of watching something wear a human face while doing something no human being has ever done.

They got the professor out. They got the documents out. They did not get a satisfying answer to the question of what Mendoza wanted, because Mendoza — having absorbed enough gunfire to kill three ordinary men — simply left. Through the window. Into the night. Walking steadily, one hand raised to his head, like a man with a mild headache.

The documents were the find. A translation of Gaspar Figueroa's Final Confessions, 1543 — a Spanish conquistador who had gone to the pyramid, taken gold from the tunnels, and paid for it. His companions turned ravenous and gaunt. Luis de Mendoza had tried to devour him. Figueroa shot his friend in the head and ran. He made it back to Lima and died the next day, hearing a voice in the dark that promised eternal life if he would only return.

Four hundred and eighty years later, the same Mendoza was still being shot in the head. Still walking it off. Still, apparently, heading toward the pyramid.

The disc of teeth on Dr. Dreyer's chest was a permanent record of what those wounds meant.

03
Episode Three — Lima

What Walks Out of Rooms

19th–20th March, 1921  |  The University • Bar Cordano • Hotel Mori

The door came off its hinges in the way that doors do when someone with Ilya's particular experience applies a boot to them. What they found on the other side was this: Mendoza, with his back to them, pinning Professor Sanchez to the floor, making a noise that no human throat ought to produce. The lamprey-mouth was open. The feeding had already begun.

Ilya shot him. Arthur shot him. Between them they put enough lead into Mendoza to settle the question for any ordinary man. He took a rifle round and three .45 shots — one of them through the skull — and he dropped, and he was still, and they moved on to the professor and the burning building and the business of getting out alive. The disc of teeth on Dr. Dreyer's chest was a permanent record of what those wounds meant. They did not linger.

The sanity cost was immediate and unkind. Ilya, face and shirt still wet with what the shots had scattered, was overtaken by the compulsion you acquire when you have seen something that moves like a living lamprey do its work at close range: wash, now, thoroughly, until the skin protests. He set the professor down in the hallway and disappeared into a bathroom and was still there, running the tap, when Inspector Rodriguez arrived with his officers.

They brought Rodriguez back to the office to show him what they had done. The body was gone. The sword was gone. The blood remained, but Mendoza — three bullets in his skull — was not there. Arthur grabbed the inspector by the lapels and described the sucking noise and the feeding and the creature's face with the focused urgency of a man who needs to be believed and knows exactly how he sounds. Rodriguez removed the hands from his overcoat with the careful deliberateness of a man who has already made up his mind.

"Senor. I believe you. There is an infestation of these creatures."

"A body would have been useful. But I'm sure the professor can clear everything up."

The doctor, sitting in the corridor outside, watched an ambulance take Professor Sanchez away — and then, across the square, caught a glimpse of Mendoza walking. Walking steadily, one hand raised to his head, like a man with a headache. Not like a man with no substantial skull remaining.

In a cab on the way back, Ilya produced the Figueroa documents. The English translation of Gaspar Figueroa's Final Confessions — sixteenth century, written on vellum — told a story they were already living. A Spanish conquistador. A pyramid southwest of Lake Titicaca. Tunnels inlaid with gold. An evil sickness. Four hundred and eighty years later, the same Mendoza was still getting shot in the head, still walking it off, and still heading toward the pyramid.

They found Larkin at the Bar Cordano. The doctor tried psychology. Five points of luck bought a hard success that said: not entirely truthful. Gold and money were not the end outcome. Outside the bar, the doctor was followed by two figures with the particular quality of stillness that belongs to things that move very fast when they choose to. The hospital is protected. Your hotel is protected. Mendoza's whereabouts are known. If you have means to leave sooner, do so.

Ilya fell asleep in a bathtub and dreamed of a jaguar moving through the sky. He was carried to a temple, and inside the temple was a tooth — ancient, gleaming, clearly meant to be somewhere specific, and equally clearly not where it was. He sat up in the bathwater and looked at himself in the mirror, and his eyes looked different. Arthur dreamed of a cold dark hand on his, resting on a switch. He woke up feeling empowered. There was a small smile on his face. He was not sure he had put it there.

The trucks leave Monday. Whatever is waiting at the pyramid has had four hundred and eighty years to be patient. It can wait until Monday.

04
Episode Four — Lima

Hunter of the Disc

21st March, 1921  |  Hotel Mori • Lima Hospital • The Racecourse

The Doctor was awake before the city was. He sat in the quiet of his room and wrote — long, spidery longhand covering page after page — setting out what he knew and what he suspected and what he could not yet account for. Three murders by three different means. No clear answer on who had shot Elias outside the hospital. The question of Mendoza's real purpose and Larkin — whether his interest in eternal life was a matter of academic curiosity or something considerably more personal. The pages filled up with facts and question marks in roughly equal measure.

He took Chema's cab to the hospital before breakfast, passing a side-street brawl without stopping. At the ward he found Luciana's dressings being changed and his medical skills told him what the nurses' prayers had not: this was no animal wound. It was a stiletto, precise and deliberate, administered by someone who wanted her alive long enough to talk. He filed the information away and collected Jackson Elias, who was discharged after his breakfast.

The cab that moved off into Lima traffic was Chema's cab but the voice from the front seat was wrong. The direction was wrong — southwest, away from the hotel, toward the racecourse — and when the Doctor said so, the driver said: "Where are you really going?" The man wanted the golden disc. He was willing to sit in that car all day with a tommy gun across his knees until he found out. He had placed a pressure-plate device beneath the seat wired to dynamite and a dead man's switch.

Back at the hotel, Chema burst through the doors in a state of grief and outrage. Ilya ran upstairs for his rifle. They borrowed Chema's friend's cab, stopped a street vendor for directions, and followed the wheel tracks to a storage area near the racecourse. Arthur trod on a cat, which announced them with the full force of its displeasure. A voice from the open ground told them to stay where they were.

The man standing in the open had a Southern European accent and a drum-fed tommy gun. Not a local. Not a robber. A specialist, clean of documentation, who had followed the disc from wherever it had started. Arthur and Ilya withdrew — and had lunch — leaving the Doctor and Jackson in the cab. On their return, Arthur rolled the disc across the floor, and the disc moved the rest of the way on its own. Arthur shot the man through his coat pocket. The kidnapper opened fire into the cab, injuring the Doctor. Ilya's rifle shot put a bullet through the centre of the forehead — releasing the dead man's switch on the dynamite.

The car exploded. The Doctor and Jackson Elias were thrown through the back seat and blown clear of the wreckage. Ilya cut the kidnapper's head off to make sure. Chema mourned his cab. Inspector Rodriguez arrived and identified the dead man as Fausto Calderón, known as The Hunter, and delivered his warning with the controlled weariness of a man professionally accustomed to foreigners causing disasters. He wanted them out of Lima by morning at the latest.

At eight o'clock the trucks rolled in. Three of them, named Niña, Pinta, Santa María. Enough dynamite to rearrange a Peruvian hillside. Arthur corrected the dangerous packing with netting. The investigators divided across the trucks and the convoy moved out.

Eight hundred miles to Puno. Then the Andes. Then whatever is in the pyramid.

The roads in 1921 are not good. Will there be bandits? What about Mendoza?

Episodes Five & Six — The Road to Puno

Eight Hundred Miles

March 1921  |  The Coast Road • The Andes • Lake Titicaca

The convoy left Lima before the city had fully woken. Three trucks in loose formation — the Niña, Pinta and Santa María — diesel smoke trailing behind them as the coast road opened south. Ilya and Jackson rode in the lead truck, eyes on the road and the treeline. Larkin and François occupied the middle. The Doctor and Arthur rode at the back with the explosives, Arthur with his legs out of the tailgate playing harmonica, the Doctor with a notebook on his knee, trying to write legibly over eight hundred miles of rough unpaved road. The sketches were illegible. The theories were not.

The journey was not uneventful. A tyre blew somewhere on the coastal stretch and Arthur, in the absence of better tools, applied his demolitions experience to the problem. With a small controlled charge and a great deal of confidence the tyre was changed. The nights on the road were watched with rotated guards, each truck posting a rifleman while the drivers slept. The Doctor, still hollowed out from the Karisiri attacks in Lima and unable to fully rest, chewed coca leaves and wrote, and tried not to think about his stolen health or the dark between the trucks.

It was during one of these night watches that the Karisiri came again. Something in the dark — not an armadillo, not the wind — and the creature was on the Doctor before he could call out, latched on and draining the life from him. The camp came awake in short order: Arthur free of his sleeping bag, quick-draw pistol in hand; Ilya dropping his torch to fire. The creature went down, not before inflicting another physical trauma on the Doctor. They burned it. The Doctor, to everyone's mild alarm, crouched over the ashes and took notes — and cut it open first, discovering an organ that collects and stores the fat drained from its victims.

The trucks made good progress after that, Arthur watching the jungle with borrowed binoculars. They reached Puno in the late afternoon of a day nobody had bothered to count, the city sitting at the edge of Lake Titicaca like something left behind by a tide. The Doctor found a tailor, had his clothes taken in, acquired a walking cane, and ate guinea pig with a scholarly appreciation of its protein-to-fat ratio. Ilya spotted they were being watched. Jackson reminded them why they had come.

Nayra lived on one of the floating reed islands of the Uros people, out on the water where the stars came down close enough to touch. They hired a boat and found her as the sun dropped behind the altiplano. She spoke through Jackson, who translated with the careful precision of a man aware that something important was happening. She told them of an ancient conflict — older than the expedition, older than memory. An evil god fell from the sky, bringing with it a great hunger. A trickster hero named Ekeko lured it underground with the promise of the world's most nutritious food, sealed the entrance with heavy stones, and instructed the people to build a pyramid over the site and bind the god with spells worked in gold.

The golden disc, Nayra said, was not a treasure. It was a tool — a reflector of the jaguar's light, made to be used against the hunger. The golden floorboard was part of a prison. A seal. It needed to go back. And somewhere in the tunnels beneath the pyramid, taken there by the Karisiri long ago, was a tooth belonging to the jaguar god Otorongo — without which the old balance could not be restored. Ilya told her about the dream. She had been waiting for someone who had seen it.

The matter of the disc was debated. Ilya said keep it — they were better at protecting it than anyone had been so far. The others agreed. Nayra and her people then treated their guests to a form of healing, restoring health and peace of mind. It was short-lived. The Karisiri came to the island, having followed the investigators from the road. The assault was short and deadly, with Ilya bitten and one of Nayra's people visibly turning into a werecat to meet them. In a hail of gunfire and knife work the creatures were felled.

The hour was late. The visitors rowed back to Puno. The truck crew was asked to stand guard so all could sleep.

800 miles by truck is over. Now comes the trek to the pyramid up into the highlands.

What are Larkin's true intentions? Where is Mendoza?
